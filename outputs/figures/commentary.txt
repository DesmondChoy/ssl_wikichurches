# Run Matrix Figure Commentary

Experiment: fine_tuning_primary_20260327
6 ViT-Base models x 3 strategies (Linear Probe, LoRA rank-8, Full) x 3 epochs
Dataset: 3,671 train / 917 val / 139 annotated eval (held out entirely)
Attention layer: 11 (final transformer block)
Statistical tests: Wilcoxon signed-rank, paired per-image


## 01 — Validation Accuracy by Model and Fine-Tuning Strategy

DINOv2 Full leads at 77.5%, but the more telling story is the gap between
Linear Probe and Full for each model — this gap measures how much a model's
frozen representation already captures style-relevant features without any
backbone adaptation.

Key takeaways:

- DINOv2 has the smallest LP->Full gap (74.8% -> 77.5%, +2.7pp), meaning its
  pretrained features are already highly style-discriminative. This becomes
  important in later figures: a model that already "knows" the task has less
  pressure to reorganize its attention.

- CLIP and SigLIP 2 have the largest gaps (63.7->73.4, +9.7pp and 66.3->72.3,
  +6.0pp). These models benefit most from backbone adaptation — their frozen
  representations are less tuned to architectural style, so fine-tuning must
  reshape internal representations more aggressively. This foreshadows why
  they also show the largest attention shifts in Figure 02.

- MAE is an outlier: even Full fine-tuning only reaches 61.5%. MAE's
  reconstruction-based pretraining produces representations that struggle with
  classification — consistent with the literature showing MAE requires longer
  fine-tuning schedules to match discriminative SSL methods.

- DINOv3 LoRA exactly matches DINOv3 Full (74.7% vs 74.7%), suggesting LoRA's
  rank-8 adaptation of Q/V projections captures nearly all learnable signal
  for this model. A practical finding: LoRA achieves parity at ~300K trainable
  parameters vs ~86M for Full.


## 02 — All-Metrics Improvement Heatmap (delta = fine-tuned - frozen)

This is the central figure. All deltas are sign-normalized so blue = improved,
red = degraded, regardless of whether the raw metric is higher-is-better
(IoU, Coverage) or lower-is-better (MSE, KL, EMD). Stars mark statistical
significance (Wilcoxon signed-rank, p < 0.05).

Key takeaways:

- The models split into two clear camps. Language-supervised models (CLIP,
  SigLIP, SigLIP 2) show consistent blue across most metrics, while
  self-supervised models (DINOv2, DINOv3, MAE) show near-zero or red. This
  is the headline finding: pretraining objective predicts whether fine-tuning
  improves attention alignment with human annotations.

- Linear Probe rows are uniformly zero across all 6 metrics — exactly as
  expected since the backbone is frozen. This serves as a methodological
  sanity check: the delta computation pipeline is working correctly, and any
  non-zero deltas for LoRA/Full reflect genuine backbone changes.

- SigLIP's Full fine-tuning shows significance stars across all 6 metrics.
  This is notable because SigLIP uses mean pooling (no CLS token), yet
  fine-tuning still systematically reshapes its spatial attention distribution.

- DINOv2 Full shows a paradox visible here: MSE *improves* (blue) while
  IoU@90 *degrades* (red). This means fine-tuning redistributes attention
  more evenly across the image (lower MSE vs ground truth), but the
  highest-attention pixels actually move *away* from annotated features.
  The attention becomes smoother but less precisely targeted.

- CLIP Full dominates KL divergence (delta = -0.76, the largest single
  improvement in the entire matrix). CLIP's frozen attention is highly
  entropic (KL = 3.69) — language-supervised pretraining produces diffuse
  attention that fine-tuning can concentrate dramatically.


## 03 — Per-Metric Diverging Bars (LoRA & Full only)

Bars sorted by delta magnitude, largest improvement at top. Linear Probe
omitted since all deltas are zero. Colors encode models for cross-metric
pattern recognition.

Key takeaways:

- CLIP Full appears at or near the top of 5 out of 6 metric panels.
  Its IoU@90 delta of +0.041 is especially striking: the frozen baseline
  IoU@90 is only ~0.018, so fine-tuning more than doubles the proportion
  of top-attended pixels overlapping with expert annotations.

- LoRA consistently achieves 50-80% of Full's improvement. For example,
  CLIP LoRA's IoU@90 delta is +0.021 vs CLIP Full's +0.041; SigLIP 2 LoRA's
  coverage delta is +0.012 vs Full's +0.015. This cost-effectiveness ratio
  is relevant for practitioners: LoRA adapts ~0.35% of parameters yet
  captures the majority of the attention alignment benefit.

- MSE tells a different story from IoU. MAE LoRA tops the MSE panel
  (delta = -0.059, p ~ 0) despite showing negligible IoU improvement. MAE's
  attention redistributes to better match the Gaussian ground-truth envelope
  without concentrating its top pixels onto the right regions. This
  highlights why multiple metrics matter — a single metric can mislead.


## 04 — IoU Delta Across Percentile Thresholds

Each panel shows one model's IoU delta at the 50th through 90th percentile,
for LoRA (blue) and Full (orange). Significance stars mark individual
thresholds. This figure reveals *how* fine-tuning reshapes the attention
distribution, not just *whether* it improves.

Key takeaways:

- CLIP Full shows a steep upward slope: delta IoU grows from +0.013 at the
  50th percentile to +0.041 at the 90th. This means fine-tuning
  disproportionately improves the *most attended* regions — the model learns
  to concentrate its peak attention onto architectural features rather than
  uniformly shifting all attention. This is the ideal behavior for
  object-level alignment.

- DINOv2 Full slopes downward: delta IoU = -0.002 at the 50th but -0.003 at
  the 90th. Fine-tuning actually degrades the highest-attention regions more
  than the mid-range ones. DINOv2's pretrained attention already peaks on
  salient structures (its frozen IoU@90 is among the highest), so
  fine-tuning disrupts an already-good pattern.

- SigLIP and SigLIP 2 both show flat positive slopes — consistent
  improvement across all thresholds (~+0.010 to +0.025). Their mean-pooling
  architecture shifts the entire attention distribution rather than
  selectively sharpening peaks, producing a qualitatively different
  improvement profile from CLIP's peak-sharpening behavior.

- DINOv3 LoRA shows a mild positive slope with significance at higher
  percentiles, while DINOv3 Full is flat near zero. LoRA's constrained
  adaptation (only Q/V projections) may act as a beneficial regularizer,
  preventing the catastrophic attention reorganization that Full allows.


## 05 — Multi-Metric Radar Profiles (normalized [0,1])

Each panel overlays the frozen baseline (gray dashed) with LoRA (blue dashed)
and Full (orange solid) for one model. All axes are normalized so outward =
better (lower-is-better metrics inverted). When a colored polygon extends
beyond the gray baseline, that strategy genuinely improved attention alignment.

Key takeaways:

- CLIP and MAE show the most dramatic polygon expansion — but for different
  reasons. CLIP expands because fine-tuning improves multiple metrics
  simultaneously (IoU, Coverage, KL, EMD). MAE expands primarily on MSE
  and KL, while its IoU axes barely move — a cautionary example of how
  radar charts can visually exaggerate improvement when it's concentrated
  on specific axes.

- DINOv2 and DINOv3's polygons nearly overlap with their frozen baselines,
  visually confirming that fine-tuning barely shifts their attention profiles.
  DINOv3's frozen polygon is already among the largest (best absolute
  performance), leaving little room for expansion.

- SigLIP and SigLIP 2 show modest but uniform expansion across all axes —
  consistent with the flat positive slopes in Figure 04. The improvement is
  real but less visually dramatic than CLIP's because it's evenly distributed
  rather than concentrated on one or two axes.

- Caution: radar polygon area is sensitive to axis ordering and can mislead.
  Use this figure for qualitative shape comparison, not quantitative claims.
  Figures 02 and 08 provide more reliable quantitative comparisons.


## 06 — Classification Accuracy vs Attention Alignment Change

The central question: does learning to classify architectural styles also
teach the model to attend to the right features? Each panel plots one model's
trajectory from Linear Probe (square) -> LoRA (diamond) -> Full (circle).
X-axis = validation accuracy, Y-axis = IoU@90 delta.

Key takeaways:

- The answer is: it depends on the model. CLIP traces a clear up-and-right
  trajectory (63.7%, delta~0 -> 73.4%, delta=+0.041), showing that
  classification gains and attention alignment improvements go hand in hand.
  SigLIP and SigLIP 2 show similar positive trajectories.

- DINOv2 delivers the sharpest counterexample: it achieves the highest
  classification accuracy in the entire experiment (77.5%) while its
  attention alignment *worsens* (delta = -0.003). High accuracy does not
  imply attention alignment with human-interpretable features. DINOv2
  can classify style correctly via features that do not correspond to
  expert-annotated architectural elements.

- This dissociation has implications for interpretability research: using
  classification accuracy as a proxy for representation quality misses
  whether the model is "right for the right reasons." DINOv2's strong
  classification but poor attention alignment suggests it exploits
  statistical shortcuts (texture, spatial frequency, background cues)
  rather than the semantic structures annotated by architectural experts.

- MAE occupies the worst quadrant — low accuracy AND near-zero attention
  improvement — confirming that reconstruction-based pretraining produces
  representations poorly suited to both classification and localization
  in this domain.


## 07 — Preserve / Enhance / Destroy Classification Matrix

The Q2 punchline in one figure. Each cell classifies a model-strategy pair
as Enhance (significant improvement, green), Preserve (no significant change,
gray), or Destroy (significant degradation, red) based on Wilcoxon signed-rank
tests with Holm correction. Linear Probe omitted (always Preserve by
definition — frozen backbone cannot shift attention).

Key takeaways:

- Across all 6 metrics and 12 model-strategy pairs: 37 Enhance, 22 Preserve,
  13 Destroy. The majority of fine-tuning interventions improve attention
  alignment, but destruction is not rare — nearly 1 in 5 combinations makes
  things significantly worse.

- CLIP Full is the only model-strategy pair that Enhances on 5 of 6 metrics
  (all except MSE, where it Destroys). This makes CLIP Full the strongest
  overall improver, but the MSE exception is important: CLIP's attention
  becomes sharper and better localized (better IoU, Coverage, KL, EMD), yet
  the pixel-level squared error against the Gaussian ground truth increases.
  Sharpening attention concentrates it into fewer pixels, increasing MSE
  against a smooth Gaussian target even when the attended location is correct.

- DINOv2 and DINOv3 are predominantly Preserve across IoU and Coverage, but
  show a mix of Enhance and Destroy on the continuous metrics (MSE, KL, EMD).
  Their attention undergoes subtle distributional shifts that don't register
  in threshold-based metrics but are detectable by distribution-sensitive
  measures.

- SigLIP and SigLIP 2 show the most consistent Enhance pattern across all
  metrics — improvement without the metric-specific trade-offs seen in CLIP.
  This uniformity suggests their mean-pooling attention shifts in a
  well-rounded way rather than optimizing one aspect at the expense of others.

- MAE's pattern is surprising: predominantly Enhance on MSE and KL (its
  attention distribution becomes more similar to ground truth) while
  Preserving on IoU and Coverage (the top-attended pixels don't move). This
  confirms the "smoother but not more targeted" behavior seen in Figure 02.


## 08 — Forest Plot with 95% Bootstrap Confidence Intervals

The academic rigor figure. Each row shows one model-strategy pair's mean
delta as a point with horizontal 95% bootstrap CI whiskers. Vertical dashed
line at zero. Sign-flipped for lower-is-better metrics so rightward always
means improvement. Stars mark significance after Holm correction.

Key takeaways:

- 50 of 72 confidence intervals exclude zero (significant after Holm
  correction). Fine-tuning produces detectable attention shifts in the
  majority of cases — this is not a null result.

- CLIP Full's IoU@90 CI is [+0.033, +0.048] — entirely right of zero with
  no overlap. This is the tightest, most clearly significant improvement in
  the IoU panels. By contrast, DINOv2 Full's IoU@90 CI is [-0.008, +0.002],
  straddling zero — consistent with its Preserve classification.

- The KL panel reveals the widest CIs and largest effect magnitudes. CLIP
  Full's KL improvement CI is [+0.70, +0.82] — an order of magnitude larger
  than the IoU effects. KL divergence is more sensitive to distributional
  shifts than threshold-based IoU, explaining why some models show
  significance on KL but not IoU.

- LoRA CIs are generally narrower than Full CIs for the same model. LoRA's
  constrained parameter space produces more predictable, lower-variance
  attention shifts across images. Full fine-tuning has more freedom, which
  introduces more per-image variability (wider CIs) even when the mean
  effect is larger.

- EMD shows the most compressed CIs — nearly all effects are small and
  tightly estimated. This reflects EMD's lower sensitivity at the 8x8
  downsampled grid used for computation, rather than a genuine lack of
  attention change.


## 09 — Per-Image Delta Strip Plot (Full Fine-Tuning)

The "show me the data" figure. Each dot is one of 139 evaluation images'
individual delta (fine-tuned minus frozen). Diamond = mean, thick bar = 95%
bootstrap CI. Dots are jittered vertically for visibility. Full fine-tuning
only (strongest signal).

Key takeaways:

- CLIP's IoU@90 improvement is remarkably consistent: the vast majority of
  dots sit right of zero, with very few negative outliers. This is not a
  mean driven by a handful of lucky images — nearly every image benefits.
  By contrast, DINOv2's dots straddle zero symmetrically, confirming that
  its near-zero mean reflects genuine ambivalence rather than cancellation
  of large positive and negative effects.

- The KL panel shows the most dramatic spread. CLIP's per-image KL deltas
  range from roughly -5 to +3 (after sign flip), with the mean at ~+0.8.
  Some individual images see enormous distributional shifts while others
  barely change. This variance is hidden by the forest plot (Figure 08),
  which only shows the mean and CI.

- SigLIP and SigLIP 2 show tight, consistently positive dot clusters across
  most metrics — the narrowest spreads among the improving models. Their
  mean-pooling attention mechanism produces highly predictable per-image
  improvements, making them the most reliable attention improvers.

- MAE's strip patterns differ strikingly across metrics: tight cluster at
  zero for IoU (nothing moves), but a wide spread for MSE and KL (the
  distribution shifts substantially on a per-image basis). This per-image
  view makes the "smoother but not more targeted" finding viscerally obvious.

- For Coverage, CLIP shows a long positive tail — a subset of images sees
  dramatic coverage increases (0.10-0.15+), likely images where frozen CLIP
  attention was entirely off-target and fine-tuning re-directed it onto
  annotated regions. These are the images that would make compelling
  qualitative examples in a presentation.
